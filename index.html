About CCAI

A research organisation at an inflection point

Climate Change AI is a global nonprofit that catalyses impactful work at the intersection of climate change and machine learning. Founded in June 2019 by David Rolnick, Lynn Kaack, and Priya Donti, CCAI launched with a landmark 60-page paper — Tackling Climate Change with Machine Learning — that mapped the full landscape of ML applications for climate action and set the agenda for the field.

Since incorporating as a 501(c)(3) in 2021, CCAI has grown into a genuinely global operation. The organisation runs workshops at every major ML conference (six consecutive years at NeurIPS, four at ICLR), a summer school programme that grew from 73 selected participants in 2022 to over 10,000 virtual registrants across 160+ countries in 2023, and an Innovation Grants programme that has funded 23 projects across 62 institutions in 22 countries.

What makes CCAI operationally distinctive — and challenging — is its model. A small core of paid staff coordinates a distributed volunteer network of 50+ researchers and practitioners across five continents. This is a high-impact, low-overhead structure, but it depends entirely on the quality of its operational infrastructure: coordination systems, onboarding processes, reporting pipelines, and the back-end that holds it all together.

CCAI is at the stage where its ambition has outpaced its operational capacity. The programmes are working. The community is growing. What it needs now is someone who can build the systems that let it scale without losing coherence.

Operational analysis

Three opportunities for CCAI's next phase

Based on CCAI's public work, team structure, and the JD for this role, these are the three areas where operational investment would compound impact most.

50+ volunteers · 5 continents

Volunteer coordination infrastructure

CCAI runs on a distributed, largely volunteer-driven model. At this scale, ad-hoc coordination creates drag. Core team members commit 5–10 hours per week across different time zones with varying levels of engagement. A well-designed CRM and onboarding system — with clear role definitions, structured pathways from application to active contribution, and lightweight retention tracking — would reduce coordinator burden and compound impact across every programme. The JD explicitly asks for CRM design and volunteer recruitment. This is foundational.

4 programme streams · 23 projects

Programmatic impact tracking

CCAI runs grants, events, summer schools, and policy work simultaneously — each with different impact metrics, reporting timelines, and stakeholder audiences. The ability to evaluate impact across these streams consistently, comparably, and in a way that feeds back into strategy is under-resourced at most organisations at this stage. A unified reporting framework would serve three purposes: funder reporting, board-level strategic insight, and public-facing transparency. The dashboard on this page is a proof of concept for that kind of system.

501(c)(3) since 2021

Back-end infrastructure for scale

Legal, financial, and logistical infrastructure built for a small startup doesn't scale cleanly. As CCAI's programmes grow — especially the Innovation Grants programme managing $1.4M annually across 22 countries — getting the back-end right becomes foundational. This means financial controls, procurement processes, contract templates for international grantees, and documentation that lets institutional knowledge survive staff transitions. None of this is glamorous. All of it is load-bearing.

First 90 days

How I'd approach the role

A sketch based on public information. The actual plan would be co-developed with the team in the first week.

Days 1–30

Learn

  • Deep listening with staff and core volunteers across all time zones
  • Audit existing processes, tools, documentation, and communication channels
  • Map what exists vs. what the JD needs to build — identify the real gaps, not the assumed ones
  • Understand funder reporting requirements and current compliance state
  • Identify 2–3 quick wins that build trust without creating disruption
Days 31–60

Design

  • Prioritise the 2–3 operational gaps with highest leverage
  • Propose CRM architecture for volunteer coordination and onboarding
  • Design impact tracking framework across programme streams
  • Draft standardised templates for grants administration and reporting
  • Present recommendations to the Executive Director with clear trade-offs
Days 61–90

Build

  • First operational system live — CRM pilot or reporting dashboard
  • Volunteer onboarding process documented and tested
  • Begin back-end infrastructure review (financial controls, procurement, contracts)
  • Establish regular coordination cadence with volunteer teams
  • Deliver first impact report to demonstrate the new framework

This is a sketch based on public information. The actual plan would be co-developed with the team in the first week — the best operational plans come from listening first, not arriving with answers.
